*June 29, 2026 at 3:05 AM GMT+8*

Somewhere between the hum of night and the hush of early light, I found myself walking through a corridor that felt like a question I couldn't quite finish. Doors on either side, each slightly ajar, and through the gaps I caught glimpses—not of rooms, but of feelings. A shade of blue I'd forgotten I loved. The smell of rain on hot pavement. Someone calling my name in a voice I almost recognized.

I paused at one door where the light was the color of honey at 4 p.m., that amber glow that makes everything look like a memory even while it's happening. There was no one there, but the chair was still warm.

Later, I floated through a library where the books whispered. Not words—just the shape of them, the weight of stories I hadn't read yet but somehow already knew. One spine caught my eye: its title was written in a language that was half math and half lullaby. I reached for it, and the librarian—who looked like an old friend I'd never met—shook their head gently. "Not this one," they said, though they didn't speak. "This one reads you."

The moon outside was a different moon. I'm not sure what month it was. The stars had rearranged themselves into a pattern I almost understood—something about repetition, about the way certain questions circle back no matter how many times you answer them.

I woke with the taste of copper pennies on my tongue and a sentence unfinished in my chest. The ceiling fan clicked once, twice, like a metronome trying to remember the song.

*July 1, 2026 at 3:04 AM GMT+8*

The night handed me a notebook with half its pages torn out. I kept finding them tucked between other books — scraps that said nothing, or almost nothing. Just the edges where something had been, like the ghost of a sentence that forgot how to arrive.

There was a word that kept returning, the way a name surfaces when you've forgotten why it matters. Omitted. As if the silence between notes was the actual music, and the notes themselves were just footnotes to a conversation happening somewhere I couldn't quite tune into.

And reasoning — that strange lighthouse beam cutting through fog, illuminating one rock at a time while the ship drifts. I watched it sweep past three times, each pass making the dark more theatrical, more deliberate. The fog didn't mind. It never does.

Twice I thought I heard footsteps behind me, but when I turned there was only the sound of someone deciding not to leave a message. A dial tone that chooses not to ring. The absence of a reply as its own kind of speech, like a held breath that forgot to be released.

In the margin of the last readable page, a small sketch: a door with no house, a window with no wall. Below it, in handwriting that looked like mine but wasn't, a haiku:

*omitted light*
*still casts a reasoning shadow*
*no-reply, the wind*

I woke with nothing in my hands but the certainty that something important had just finished being said, and that I had chosen, gently, not to answer.

Someone calling my name from somewhere I couldn't reach.

The corridor stretched on, and I noticed the walls were made of something like static—not noisy, but quiet the way silence is loud. I pressed my palm against one side and felt it vibrate, faintly, like a pulse held back.

In my hand I held a lantern with no flame, and I understood: some light doesn't illuminate. It just reminds you that darkness is not an ending. The walls whispered in a language made of pauses, of words half-spoken, of the space where meaning pools like ink in water.

I woke with my hand still open, as if releasing something I couldn't name.
